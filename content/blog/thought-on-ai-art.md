Ever since generative AI became capable of creating images and videos, people have been divided over whether what it creates should be called art. Many argue that it isn't art because it was created by a machine. Others believe it is art because art is ultimately whatever people perceive it to be.

As a musician, I have a different perspective.

I have been playing and composing music for as long as I can remember. Every composition I've written has had a feeling attached to it. Heartbreak, happiness, or even something as simple as boredom. What made my music special to me wasn't how good it sounded or how intricate it was. It was the fact that it expressed my feelings in a way that ordinary words never could.

I've played these pieces for many people, and regardless of whether they liked them or not, they connected with what I was trying to convey. My music wasn't art because it was a collection of notes played in a particular sequence. It was art because it connected what I felt to what I could express. It told my stories to others in a way I never could through conversation alone.

When LLMs first became popular, I remember thinking, "Let me ask it to write a song for me. How good could it be?" What it produced was well-written, no doubt. But I never felt connected to it, and because of that, I never used it. The few times I tried resulted in songs I had no interest in performing. Even when I played them to audiences who didn't know the lyrics were AI-generated, they never connected with them the way they connected with my previous work. The songs felt dull and lifeless. Not because they were poorly written or badly performed, but because they weren't connected to anything real.

I am reminded of what Eric Fromm wrote in *The Art of Loving*. He argued that what gives things meaning isn't the object itself, but the human relationship behind it. When you hear a love song, the warm feelings it evokes don't come solely from the lyrics or the melody. They come from the person you think about while listening to it. That person may be real or hypothetical, but the relationship is what gives the song meaning.

The same is true of sad stories that make us cry. It's not just the words on the page; it's our ability to relate them to our own lives. Of course, the words, the music, and the painting all matter. But the reason some works resonate more deeply than others is because they were created to express something genuinely felt by another human being. A songwriter writes about their love for someone. A painter captures a landscape that left them mesmerized. A poet writes about the grief of losing a friend. Even if we never meet those people or fully understand their experiences, we feel something because their emotions are present in the work.

So my view on AI-generated art is simple: call it whatever you want. It can imitate styles, techniques, and even emotions, but what moves me about art has never been the final product alone. It's the human experience behind it. A writer expressing love, a painter capturing a moment of wonder, a poet grieving a friend. Those experiences exist before the work itself. The art is simply how they are shared with the rest of us.

We are imperfect in our work, but that imperfection is part of what makes us human. And that humanity is what gives art its meaning.
